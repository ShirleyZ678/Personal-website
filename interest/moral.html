Moral cognition refers to cognitive processes that allow a person to act or decide in morally permissible ways. It consists of several domain-general cognitive processes, ranging from perception of a morally salient stimulus to reasoning when faced with a moral dilemma. While it's important to mention that there is not a single cognitive faculty dedicated exclusively to moral cognition,[31][32] characterizing the contributions of domain-general processes to moral behavior is a critical scientific endeavor to understand how morality works and how it can be improved.

The Footbridge Dilemma A trolley car (or tram) is out of control. If it continues along the current tracks it will crash into five hikers crossing the line. There is a footbridge going over the track with a fat man standing on it. If you push the fat man off the footbridge on to the line it will kill him, but it will stop the train before it gets to the five hikers, thus saving them. You are standing next to the fat man. What do you do?
